Addressing Diversity Through the Years

“It’s society that needs to change its ways, not the individual who needs to be fixed."

Reading about the history of how people with disabilities were treated made me feel very sad and a bit heavy-hearted. It’s hard to swallow the fact that, for a long time, people were seen as "broken" or even as a punishment from God. I felt a mix of anger and regret for how society used to shut people out just because they didn't fit a certain mold. It’s a painful history, but it’s one that I think we really need to face so we don't repeat it.

I realized that the way we define "disability" has changed so much, and for the better. The shift from the medical model to the social model was a huge lightbulb momentfor me. I learned that it’s often not the person’s condition that limits them, but the stairs we build, the narrow doors we create, and the unfair attitudes we hold. It’s society that needs to change its ways, not the individual who needs to be fixed to fit in.

This lesson made me want to be an advocate for fairness. It affected me by making me realize that I have a responsibility to speak up when I see someone being excluded. I can apply this by looking at my environment—whether it's my school or my neighborhood—and asking, Who is being left out here? I want to help build a world where everyone has a seat at the table, not because we’re being nice, but because it is their right to be there.
